Trojan Horses Awareness

Understand how Trojan horses work, how to recognize them, and how to protect your system from these cyber threats.

What are Trojan Horses?

A Trojan horse (or "Trojan") is a type of malicious software that disguises itself as a legitimate program or file. Once installed, it can cause severe damage, steal sensitive data, or provide backdoor access to cybercriminals.

How Do Trojan Horses Work?

The mechanism of a Trojan typically follows these steps:

  1. The attacker sends a seemingly harmless email or message with an attachment or a link to a malicious file.
  2. The victim downloads and executes the file, which appears to be a legitimate program or document.
  3. The Trojan installs itself in the background without the user's knowledge.
  4. Once activated, it can steal data, damage files, or give attackers control over the device.

  • Rootkit Trojan: Hides the presence of malware and enables remote access without being detected.

How to Spot a Trojan Horse

  • Unusual system behavior, such as slow performance, crashes, or unresponsive programs.
  • Unexpected network traffic or the presence of unknown files or programs running in the background.
  • Pop-up messages or alerts that appear unexpectedly, often requesting you to install software or click suspicious links.
  • Unauthorized access or changes to personal files and data.

What Should You Do if You Suspect a Trojan?

  • Disconnect your device from the internet to prevent further damage or data theft.
  • Run a full system scan with updated antivirus or anti-malware software to detect and remove the Trojan.
  • Check for suspicious programs or files in your system and remove anything unknown or unnecessary.
  • Change passwords for important accounts, especially if personal data has been compromised.
  • Report the incident to your organization's IT department or to cybersecurity authorities.

How to Protect Yourself from Trojan Horses

  • Install and regularly update reputable antivirus or anti-malware software.
  • Be cautious when opening email attachments or clicking on links from unknown or suspicious sources.
  • Keep your operating system, browsers, and software up to date to avoid vulnerabilities.
  • Use strong, unique passwords and enable two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever possible.
  • Always download software from trusted, official sources and avoid pirated or cracked programs.
